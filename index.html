HOW COVID-19 CHANGED LIFE IN EUROPE

Trinity Group

By Alessio Distante and Hanna De Maria

INTRODUCTION

The SARS-CoV-2 epidemic, better known as Covid-19, which took hold in the early months of 2020, caught mankind completely unprepared.

It had been a century since an epidemic of this magnitude had been seen, and the highly globalized world we live in today allowed the virus to spread at an uncontrollable rate. In a matter of weeks, the entire globe realized that extreme measures were needed for containment, and that most healthcare systems were not prepared to handle such an enormous influx of patients. To prevent the spread of the virus, many nations adopted measures of social distancing, closure of non-essential businesses, and travel restrictions. These measures have had, and continue to have, a strong impact on our society, economy, and healthcare systems.

The goal is to analyze the key sectors, particularly those in Europe, that have undergone significant changes due to the epidemic, examining their causes and consequences. Specifically, we will analyze:

  • Healthcare sector
  • Economic sector
  • Social sector
  • Environmental sector

    Europe has been one of the hardest hit areas. Data on deaths in individual countries can be seen in the circular barplot on the right.

    The ease with which one can travel from one country to another, especially within the European Union thanks to the Schengen treaty, is certainly a contributing factor. In fact, excluding Russia and the United Kingdom, which initially approached the epidemic rather lightly, the countries that have suffered the most are precisely the major players in the European Union.

    This is a consequence of the fact that, in a highly interconnected world where the exchange of people and goods is constant and extremely rapid, it is difficult to paralyze entire nations and in the meantime, as appropriate measures are taken, the virus has had an enormous amount of time to spread.

    Such a high number of deaths is also due to the inadequacy of the majority of national healthcare systems in dealing with an epidemic of this magnitude, both in terms of structure (personnel, beds, etc.) and in terms of medical supplies (masks, gloves). In particular, the most glaring and serious lack has been that of intensive care beds, which are necessary to stabilize people in critical condition thanks to ventilators.     Moreover, as researched and confirmed, Covid-19 was found to be extremely more dangerous as age increases and for those with pre-existing conditions, such as cardiovascular diseases, diabetes, hypertension, and especially lung disorders.

    There are several reasons why COVID-19 may be more dangerous for these people:

    • Compromised immune system: older people and those with previous illnesses often have a weakened immune system, which makes it more difficult for them to fight the virus.
    • Higher risk of complications: this category of people is more susceptible to developing complications such as pneumonia, respiratory failure and organ failure.
    • Viral load: it has been shown that these are people who may have a higher viral load, which means that their body is infected with more virus. This can increase the severity of symptoms and the risk of complications.
    • Defects in the immune response: in some cases, the immune system of elderly people and those with previous illnesses may respond excessively to infection, causing inflammation and tissue damage.



    The doughnut chart on the right clearly shows the difference in the danger of the virus according to age, but above all that the number of deaths is directly proportional to increasing age.

    The governments of almost all Europe therefore decided to implement drastic measures to contain the uncontrolled spread of the virus.

    In the above map, it is possible to observe three different measures: internal movement restrictions, testing policies, and mask policies. Regarding testing policies and mask policies, almost all of Europe moved cohesively by adopting similar safety measures and restrictions to limit the spread of the virus. However, there were some variations among different countries based on the local epidemiological situation, available resources, and political preferences. For example, some countries imposed stricter restrictions than others, and some nations made mask-wearing mandatory in indoor public places, while others did not.
    Regarding internal movement restrictions, after the peak of the epidemic, many countries decided to remove them with not always positive results.

    These measures were of fundamental importance to contain the virus and limit the damage, but certainly could not be a long-term solution. The entire scientific community agreed that the only feasible solution to definitively leave the epidemic behind would be the vaccine. And so it was: as is possible to observe in the graph below, which relates deaths, cases, and vaccinations, the impressive vaccination campaign allowed to keep the number of deaths under control.

    In fact, the correlation between the increase in vaccinations and the decrease in the number of deaths is clear, even though the number of cases remained unchanged or, in some cases, was extremely higher than in previous periods (such as in the first part of 2022). This phenomenon can be explained by the vaccines' ability to prevent severe cases and deaths, even if they do not entirely prevent infection. Moreover, the fact that the number of cases remained unchanged or even increased in some periods could be due to the vaccines not being administered to the entire population and the existence of virus variants that can spread more easily. However, despite these factors, the increase in vaccinations was a crucial element to contain the virus's spread and reduce the number of deaths.

    ECONOMY

    All the restrictions resulted in a major slowdown in the global and European economies. The pandemic has carved a very deep furrow in the economies of all countries and the World Bank has estimated that the Covid-19 epidemic has triggered one of the worst economic recessions since 1870, bringing with it a dramatic increase in poverty levels.

    A drop in GDP ('Gross Domestic Product') can have negative consequences on employment, incomes and people's quality of life, as it can lead to fewer jobs, lower wages and a decrease in overall economic well-being.
    By the end of 2020, the world economy experienced a GDP decline (relative to 2019) of 5.2 %. Furthermore, while international trade had grown by 1.1 per cent in 2019, it fell by about 11 percentage points in 2020.

    The decrease in trade, and consequently lower productivity, had a strong impact on the nations' exports. The export figures for the four most populous European countries can be seen in the graph below, and the drastic decrease in exports due to the pandemic is clearly visible. It can also be seen that most countries are struggling to return to pre-pandemic levels.

    GDP AND INFLATION OF EUROPEAN UNION

    TOOLTIP: mouse over line or legend for highlight a specific line

    The data on GDP and inflation within the European Union can be seen in the graph on the right, and as expected, it is not positive.

    In fact, a decrease in trade was followed by a sharp drop in the gross domestic product (GDP) of most European countries. GDP is the value of goods and services produced within a sovereign state over a certain period of time and is one of the major indicators of the health of a country's economy.

    An extremely interesting piece of data is also that relating to inflation. In fact, during the pandemic, in a seemingly counterintuitive way, inflation has decreased. This has happened for three main reasons:

    1. Firstly, the significant decrease in oil prices, which fell from around $70 per barrel at the beginning of 2020 to less than $20 at the end of April 2020.
    2. Secondly, the pandemic has also led to a reallocation of spending on consumption among different categories, with a collapse in spending on tourism, travel, and hospitality, and, conversely, an increase in spending on household goods, including food and equipment for working, studying, and exercising at home.
    3. Thirdly, some governments have introduced temporary reductions in VAT rates.


    But a decrease in inflation is not always a positive thing. Excessively low inflation, as well as excessively high inflation, have serious effects on a country's economy.

    For example, if inflation falls too much, interest rates decrease, and a massive drop in prices ensues. If people expect prices to fall, they will be tempted to postpone their spending until that time: this will lead to a sharp drop in consumption and it is also less likely that people will request loans since they do not need credit to make purchases: their money allows them to buy enough goods. If loans or mortgages are not requested, no new money is created, and the economy stalls.

    Conversely, if inflation grows too much, the value of money is eroded and the cost of living becomes too high to continue to support a healthy economy, since people will not be able to sustain any other expenses beyond basic necessities, and all their money will be consumed by fixed expenses necessary for subsistence; this also means that far fewer people will be able to save or invest.

    While some sectors have suffered disastrous setbacks, such as the tourism, theatre or film industries, others have benefited from the pandemic. In particular, it is mostly those sectors dealing with Internet and Multimedia, Telecommunications and the 'online' world in general.

    This is a consequence of a simple fact: the imposing restrictions have forced virtually every activity online, from education, to work, to public services, to move.

    In the graph on the left, one can observe the relative market share of five sectors before and during covid. It is possible to observe how the sectors related to tourism, i.e. the hotel and travel agency sector, despite their efforts to adapt to the situation by offering online booking services and developing new ways of offering their services, plummeted, leaving room for technology sectors such as telecommunications and the ICT sector in general.

    The fact that the demand shock caused by the crisis did not affect all sectors equally causes strong asymmetries, which could persist even in the long run. It is possible that such a situation will continue due to several factors: the rise of online trade could become permanent, smart working could lead to a collapse of catering and transport services, and in general it is likely that the demand for all those services involving physical proximity (concerts, holidays) will decrease in the medium or long term, given the particular nature of Covid-19 risks.

    Despite some sectors having emerged stronger, the economy as a whole has suffered a significant slowdown and a significant slowdown in the economy inevitably falls on the population.

    The slowdown in the economy has led to a decrease in trade, which has led to a decrease in the amount of money in circulation. This in turn has caused a decrease in consumption, which in turn has reduced the need for labor to produce goods and services. This has led to layoffs and a decrease in employment.

    The situation is particularly worrying when considering the employment data of the four most populous countries in Europe. The data in the graph on the right shows how the pandemic has had a dramatic effect on employment in these countries, to the extent that the loss of jobs has been greater than that recorded during the global financial crisis of 2008-2010.

    Among the categories of workers most affected by the emergency were temporary workers, young workers, and low-wage female workers. These workers have been more severely affected by the pandemic, as they have less social protection and job security than other workers. Furthermore, the pandemic has exposed the precariousness of employment in Europe and highlighted the need for greater social protection for workers.

    Secondo i risultati di uno studio realizzato dal Dipartimento di Scienze Biomediche di Humanitas University, la pandemia di Covid-19 ha impattato in maniera significativa sulla sfera psicologica ed emozionale degli individui. Questo studio preliminare, basato si di un campione di 2.400 persone, ha rilevato che nel periodo di pandemia il 21% degli intervistati ha notato un peggioramento nei rapporti con il partner e il 13% con i propri figli.     ENVIRONMENT

    Despite the enormous difficulties and obstacles faced during the epidemic, it is possible to identify some positive aspects that Covid-19 has brought with it. The restrictive measures adopted to combat the spread of the pandemic have generated some positive short-term effects on the European environment.

    The almost complete halt of production activities in various sectors has led to a collapse in demand for electricity, gas, and oil. At the same time, the restrictions on national and international travel have led to a drastic reduction in the number of trips made by all types of transportation, including air, road, and rail. This has resulted in a significant decrease in carbon dioxide (CO2) and other greenhouse gas emissions produced by transportation, which represent approximately 16.2% of global greenhouse gas emissions (data updated to 2016).

    One of the most extraordinary short-term effects has been the reduction of global greenhouse gas emissions. In 2020 alone, Europe saw a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions of 7.6%.

    The Covid-19 pandemic has also had positive consequences for air quality, especially in the most polluted cities. The almost total interruption of transportation due to virus containment measures has had a significant impact on the concentrations of pollutants such as nitrogen dioxide (NO2) and fine particles (PM10), which are highly harmful to human health and contribute to air pollution. In some European cities, such as Milan and Madrid, there has been a 70% reduction in the concentrations of these elements, which has resulted in a significant improvement in air quality.

    On average, during lockdown periods, electricity demand has been 20% lower compared to the same period in 2019, which is also the lowest recorded in recent years.

    In recent years, the environmental issue has become increasingly urgent and of great global interest. Pollution is one of the most serious environmental problems afflicting the planet and has a negative impact on human health and biodiversity.

    The decrease in pollution has had significant effects across the globe: In many places around the world, there has been a significant reduction in pollution levels. For example, in India, the pollution level of the country's largest freshwater lake has decreased by 16%, as reported in a recent report. This shows how decreasing human activities, such as vehicular traffic and industrial activities, can have a positive impact on the environment.
    Moreover, beaches and the marine environment have also benefited from the lockdown. In fact, as a result of beach closures and reduced travel, there has been a decrease in the amount of litter entering the marine environment off the coast of Kenya. This is a major achievement as marine litter poses a threat to marine wildlife and the coastal ecosystem.

    CONCLUSIONS

    Il lavoro di ricerca e analisi dati presentato ha permesso di avere una comprensione un po' più approfondita dell'impatto della pandemia sui diversi settori della società.

    Per quanto riguarda il settore sanitario, è emerso come la pandemia abbia messo a dura prova i sistemi sanitari di molti paesi, con una forte richiesta di risorse umane e materiali. La ricerca ha anche messo in evidenza l'importanza delle misure preventive, come il distanziamento sociale e l'uso delle mascherine, nella riduzione della diffusione del virus. E' inoltre emerso il ruolo essenziale del vaccino nello sconfiggere quasi definitivamente l'epidemia.

    Nel settore economico, la ricerca ha evidenziato gli effetti negativi della pandemia, come la riduzione del Pil, la perdita di posti di lavoro, la chiusura di molte attività commerciali e la drastica diminuzione dei commerci. E' emerso inoltre come non tutti i settori economici siano stati colpiti allo stesso modo, così come le categorie sociali.

    La ricerca ha anche analizzato gli effetti della pandemia sulla società. Le misure di distanziamento sociale hanno portato a un aumento della solitudine e dell'isolamento, specialmente tra i giovani. La pandemia ha anche messo in evidenza le disuguaglianze sociali, con i gruppi svantaggiati che hanno subito maggiormente gli effetti negativi della pandemia. Tuttavia, la pandemia ha anche portato a un aumento dell'attenzione da parte della comunità scientifica, e non solo, sulle questioni sociali, come la salute mentale, l'accesso alle risorse e l'uguaglianza.

    Infine, la ricerca ha analizzato gli effetti della pandemia sull'ambiente, evidenziando come la riduzione delle attività economiche e dei viaggi abbia portato a una diminuzione delle emissioni di gas serra e ad un miglioramento della qualità di aria e acqua.

    In conclusione, nonostante l'impatto devastante della pandemia di COVID-19 sulla salute pubblica e sull'economia globale, c'è motivo di speranza per il futuro. La risposta globale alla pandemia è stata senza precedenti, con la collaborazione tra paesi, organizzazioni internazionali e scienziati che ha portato allo sviluppo di vaccini altamente efficaci in un tempo record. Inoltre, la pandemia ha portato a una rapida adozione di tecnologie avanzate, come la telemedicina e il lavoro a distanza, che potrebbero portare a una maggiore efficienza e flessibilità nei sistemi sanitari e aziendali in futuro.

    Siamo stati in grado di adattarci e trovare nuove soluzioni per proteggere la nostra salute e il nostro benessere mentale durante questa crisi. La pandemia ha anche messo in evidenza l'importanza di investire nella ricerca scientifica e nella preparazione per future crisi sanitarie.

    In sintesi, la pandemia di COVID-19 ci ha insegnato importanti lezioni e ci ha spinto a cercare soluzioni innovative. Con la collaborazione e la determinazione, possiamo affrontare le sfide future e costruire un futuro più sano e resiliente per tutti.
